How we teach a concept

Instead of memorizing formulas, students learn when a formula is useful, why it is true, and how it changes under constraints. For example, the identity \((a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2\) becomes a counting story, an area story, and an inequality tool.

Number Theory

Divisibility, modular arithmetic, Euclidean algorithm, valuations, and Diophantine equations. Example: if \(a\equiv b\pmod m\), then \(a^k\equiv b^k\pmod m\).

Algebra

Identities, factorization, inequalities, sequences, and functional equations. Students learn to transform expressions like \(x^2+y^2\geq 2xy\) into problem-solving tools.

Geometry

Angle chasing, congruence, similarity, cyclic quadrilaterals, power of a point, and coordinate geometry. We connect diagrams to proof structure.

Combinatorics

Counting, bijections, pigeonhole principle, invariants, parity, and recursion. Example recurrence: \(F_n=F_{n-1}+F_{n-2}\).

Sample mini-lesson: Modular arithmetic

When a problem asks about divisibility, residues often simplify the situation. To prove \(n^5-n\) is divisible by \(5\), check residues \(n\equiv 0,1,2,3,4\pmod 5\). This turns a statement about infinitely many integers into five small cases.

Core idea: \[\text{large number} = \text{quotient}\times m + \text{remainder}.\]
