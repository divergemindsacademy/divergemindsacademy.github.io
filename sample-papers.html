Set A

Comparatively Easy Problem Set

AlgebraNested Radical
A1

The infinite radical

Let

\[N=\sqrt{2026+2025\sqrt{2026+2025\sqrt{2026+2025\sqrt{\cdots}}}}.\]

It turns out that \(N\) is a positive integer. Find the sum of the digits of \(N\).

Call the entire radical \(N\). The expression after the first square root is again \(N\).

We have \(N^2=2026+2025N\), so \(N^2-2025N-2026=0\). This factors as \((N-2026)(N+1)=0\). Since \(N>0\), \(N=2026\). The required digit sum is \(2+0+2+6=10\).

RatesAverage Speed
A2

Three equal road segments

The road from Town \(A\) to Town \(B\) has three equal-length parts: uphill, flat, and downhill. A cyclist travels at \(8\) km/h uphill, \(12\) km/h on the flat part, and \(24\) km/h downhill. What is the average speed for the whole journey?

Do not average the speeds directly. Let each segment have length \(d\), then add the times.

Total distance is \(3d\). Total time is \(\frac d8+\frac d{12}+\frac d{24}=\frac{3d+2d+d}{24}=\frac d4\). Hence the average speed is \(\frac{3d}{d/4}=12\) km/h.

SequencesTelescoping Product
A3

A recursively controlled sequence

Let \(a_1=2026\), and for \(n\geq2\),

\[a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n=n^2a_n.\]

Find the value of \(2025a_{2025}\).

Subtract the equation for \(n-1\) from the equation for \(n\). A simple recurrence for \(a_n\) appears.

Since \(a_n=(a_1+\cdots+a_n)-(a_1+\cdots+a_{n-1})\), we get \(a_n=n^2a_n-(n-1)^2a_{n-1}\). Thus \((n^2-1)a_n=(n-1)^2a_{n-1}\), so \(a_n=\frac{n-1}{n+1}a_{n-1}\). Therefore \(a_{2025}=\frac{2a_1}{2025\cdot2026}=\frac{2}{2025}\), and \(2025a_{2025}=2\).

GeometryArea
A4

Two trisectors and an intersection

In the unit square \(ABCD\), points \(P,Q\) lie on \(BC\) such that \(BP=PQ=QC\). Let \(R\) be the intersection of \(AQ\) and \(DP\). If \([\triangle PQR]=\frac1N\), find \(N\).

Drop a perpendicular from \(R\) to \(BC\). By symmetry, it lands at the midpoint of \(PQ\).

Let \(S\) be the foot of the perpendicular from \(R\) to \(BC\). By symmetry, \(PS=\frac16\). Since \(RS\parallel DC\), triangles \(PRS\) and \(PDC\) are similar. Hence \(\frac{RS}{1}=\frac{PS}{PC}=\frac{1/6}{2/3}=\frac14\). Therefore \([\triangle PQR]=\frac12\cdot\frac13\cdot\frac14=\frac1{24}\), so \(N=24\).

CombinatoricsInclusion-Exclusion
A5

Half-palindromes

A six-digit number \(abcdef\) is called a half-palindrome if either the first three digits \(abc\) or the last three digits \(def\) form a palindrome. What percentage of all six-digit numbers are half-palindromes?

Count: first block palindromic, second block palindromic, then subtract the overlap.

There are \(900000\) six-digit numbers. A palindromic first block \(abc\) has \(90\) choices and \(def\) has \(1000\) choices, giving \(90000\). Similarly the last block palindromic gives \(90000\). The overlap has \(90\cdot100=9000\) choices. Thus the count is \(90000+90000-9000=171000\), which is \(19\%\) of \(900000\).

Set B

Hard Problem Set

These are designed to look innocent but reward spotting the right structure.

AlgebraPolynomial Interpolation
B1

A polynomial hiding a zero

Let \(P(x)\) be a polynomial of degree \(2024\) such that

\[P(n)=\frac{n}{n+1}\quad\text{for }n=0,1,2,\ldots,2024.\]

Find \(P(2026)\).

Construct \(Q(x)=(x+1)P(x)-x\). It vanishes at many consecutive integers.

Define \(Q(x)=(x+1)P(x)-x\). Then \(Q\) has degree \(2025\), and \(Q(0)=Q(1)=\cdots=Q(2024)=0\). Hence \(Q(x)=k x(x-1)\cdots(x-2024)\). Also \(Q(-1)=1\), so \(1=k(-1)(-2)\cdots(-2025)=-k\cdot2025!\), giving \(k=-1/2025!\). Then \(Q(2026)=k\cdot2026!=-2026\). Therefore \(P(2026)=\frac{Q(2026)+2026}{2027}=0\).

Number TheoryDigit Reversal
B2

A reversed three-digit number

Let \(N\) be a three-digit integer that does not end in zero. Define \(R(N)\) as the three-digit integer obtained by reversing the digits of \(N\). For example, \(R(137)=731\).

How many such integers \(N\) satisfy

\[R(N)=2N+5?\]

Write \(N=100a+10b+c\), where \(a,c\in\{1,2,\ldots,9\}\) and \(b\in\{0,1,\ldots,9\}\). Then compare the equation digit-by-digit through one linear equation.

Let \(N=100a+10b+c\). Then \(R(N)=100c+10b+a\). The condition gives \(100c+10b+a=2(100a+10b+c)+5\), hence \(98c=199a+10b+5\). The right side is even only when \(a\) is odd. If \(a\ge5\), then \(199a+10b+5>98\cdot9\), impossible. So check \(a=1,3\). For \(a=1\), \(98c=204+10b\), which works only at \(c=3,b=9\), giving \(N=193\). For \(a=3\), no digit \(c\) gives a valid \(b\). Therefore exactly \(1\) such integer exists.

GeometryCyclic + Semicircles
B3

The shaded region tells the triangle

Triangle \(ABC\) is right-angled at \(C\). Let \(\Omega\) be its circumcircle, and let \(D\) be a point on \(\Omega\), not on the same semicircular arc made by \(AB\) as \(C\). Semicircles are drawn on \(AC,BC,BD,AD\). If \([\triangle ABC]=24\) and the total shaded region has area \(42\), find \([\triangle ABD]\).

Use the lune idea: in a right triangle, the two small semicircles together match the semicircle on the hypotenuse, except for the triangle area.

For a right triangle, the shaded lune area built on the two legs equals the area of the triangle. So the shaded part above \(AB\) equals \([\triangle ABC]=24\). Similarly, because \(\angle ADB=90^\circ\), the shaded part below \(AB\) equals \([\triangle ABD]\). The total shaded area is \(42\), so \([\triangle ABD]=42-24=18\).

CombinatoricsRegions
B4

Beautifully placed circles

A collection of circles in the plane is called beautifully placed if every pair of circles intersects at two points and no point lies on three circles. Suppose \(2026\) circles are beautifully placed and divide the plane into \(N\) regions. Find the number formed by the last two digits of \(N\).

Try drawing smaller cases first: \(n=1,2,3,4\). Count the regions, look for the pattern in how many new regions each added circle creates, and then try to prove that pattern.

We denote by \(P(n)\) the number of regions divided by \(n\) beautifully placed circles. For small values, we have \(P(1)=2\), \(P(2)=4\), \(P(3)=8\), \(P(4)=14\), and from this we notice that

\[\begin{aligned} P(1)&=2,\\ P(2)&=P(1)+2,\\ P(3)&=P(2)+4,\\ P(4)&=P(3)+6,\\ &\vdots\\ P(n)&=P(n-1)+2(n-1). \end{aligned}\]

Summing these equations, we obtain

\[P(n)=2+2+4+\cdots+2(n-1)=2+n(n-1).\]

This formula can be shown by induction on \(n\) to hold true. The base case \(n=1\) is obvious.

For the induction step, assume that the formula holds for \(n=k\geq1\), i.e., \(P(k)=2+k(k-1)\). Consider \(k+1\) circles. The \((k+1)\)-st circle intersects the previous \(k\) circles at \(2k\) points, since it cuts each of them twice. This divides the new circle into \(2k\) arcs, and each arc divides one existing region into two sub-regions. Therefore, we get \(2k\) additional regions.

\[P(k+1)=P(k)+2k=2+k(k-1)+2k=2+k(k+1).\]

The proof by induction is complete. Using this result for \(n=2026\),

\[N=2+2026(2026-1)=2+2026\cdot2025.\]

Clearly, \(N\equiv2+26\cdot25=2+650\equiv52\pmod{100}\). Therefore, the number formed by the last two digits of \(N\) is \(52\).

Number TheorySquares
B5

When a triangular-square expression is square

Find the product of the digits of the smallest integer \(n\ge2\) such that

\[\sqrt{\frac{(n+1)(2n+1)}6}\]

is an integer.

First force divisibility by \(6\), so \(n\equiv1\) or \(5\pmod6\). Then split into cases.

Let the expression equal \(m\). Then \((n+1)(2n+1)=6m^2\), so \(n\equiv1\) or \(5\pmod6\). If \(n=6k+5\), then \(m^2=(k+1)(12k+11)\), and the coprime factors must both be squares, leading to \(12a^2=b^2+1\), impossible modulo \(4\). If \(n=6k+1\), then \(m^2=(3k+1)(4k+1)\). Taking \(3k+1=a^2\), \(4k+1=b^2\), we get \(3b^2=(2a-1)(2a+1)\). The first nontrivial working value is \(a=13\), giving \(3b^2=25\cdot27\), so \(b=15\), \(k=56\), and \(n=337\). The digit product is \(3\cdot3\cdot7=63\).
